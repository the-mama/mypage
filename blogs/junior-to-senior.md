# From Junior to Senior: The Skills They Never Teach

## The Promotion Narrative Nobody Talks About

Your first promotion is usually about writing better code.

Your second promotion is rarely about code at all.

I spent my first 3 years optimizing algorithms and thinking "I'm not senior yet because I haven't mastered the hardest technical problems."

Then I got promoted, and it had nothing to do with that.

## Skill 1: Reading the Room

Senior engineers ship changes that survive a meeting.

Junior engineers ship changes that fail in production.

The difference? Anticipating how others will break your code.

**How I do this:**
- Before implementing, I think: "What assumptions am I making?"
- I ask the team: "What could go wrong with this approach?"
- I design for the 80% case, not the 20% gotcha

**Real example:** Proposed a new data model to the analytics team. Before coding, I asked "When would this break?" Caught a timezone issue nobody had thought of.

## Skill 2: Saying No (Respectfully)

Juniors say yes to everything and burn out.

Seniors say no strategically.

**What I learned:**
- "Let me scope this first" (buys you thinking time)
- "This conflicts with X. Which matters more?" (forces prioritization)
- "If we do this, we can't do Y by the deadline" (exposes real trade-offs)

**Real impact:** Prevented 3 projects that would have failed due to unrealistic scope.

## Skill 3: Explaining Complex Things Simply

If you can't explain it in 2 minutes, you don't understand it.

I used to write 10-page technical designs.

Now I write 1-page summaries with the hard questions answered:
- Why are we doing this?
- What could go wrong?
- How do we know it worked?

**The test:** Can a non-engineer understand it?

If yes, an engineer can definitely understand it.

## Skill 4: Knowing What You Don't Know

Juniors pretend to know everything (imposter syndrome, proving themselves).

Seniors openly say "I'm not sure, let me research that."

**What changed for me:**
- I started saying "I don't know" in meetings (people respected it more)
- I built networks to ask hard questions (instead of staying stuck)
- I learned faster because I wasn't wasting energy on pretending

**Real example:** Didn't know Azure Fabric when hired. Openly stated it, then ran a knowledge-sharing session 2 weeks later. Respected more for learning, not for pretending I knew.

## Skill 5: Building vs. Fixing

Juniors build features.

Seniors build systems that don't need fixing.

**The difference:**
- Juniors: "How do I make this work?"
- Seniors: "How do I make sure this doesn't break?"

This includes:
- Monitoring from day 1
- Error handling upfront
- Code that's easy to debug
- Documentation that actually helps

## Skill 6: Influencing Without Authority

You don't have permission to change how the team works, but you want to.

**Strategies I use:**
- Propose small experiments ("Let's try this for 2 weeks")
- Show results, not ideology ("This pattern reduced bugs by 40%")
- Make it easy to adopt ("I made a template, you can copy it")
- Don't die on hills ("This convention isn't critical")

**Real impact:** Introduced medallion architecture, dbt, and monitoring—not by mandate, but by showing they work.

## Skill 7: Mentoring Without Saying "Let Me Teach You"

This is 50% of the senior role.

Instead of pointing out what's wrong:
- Ask: "What approach were you thinking?"
- Listen
- Ask: "What could go wrong?"
- Let them find the issues

They learn 10x faster, and they own the solution.

## The Uncomfortable Truth

Being senior isn't about knowing more technical things.

It's about:
- Making others better
- Preventing problems instead of solving them
- Communicating clearly
- Knowing when to push and when to let go
- Building trust

## How I Actually Got There

**Year 1-2:** "I'm the best engineer on this team at coding"
(Imposter syndrome, not true)

**Year 2-3:** "I ship things that work"
(Still thinking like an IC)

**Year 3-4:** "I make the team better"
(That's when the promotion came)

**Year 4-5:** "I prevent fires instead of fighting them"
(This is when I became actually valuable)

## The Promotion Question

If someone asks "Why haven't I been promoted?", 90% of the time it's not technical.

It's one of these:
- They don't communicate their impact
- They don't help others succeed
- They're not building the systems; they're just building features
- They don't anticipate what's coming next

## What Matters in the Room

In a room with 5 senior engineers and 5 junior engineers:

The senior engineers don't impress by solving hard technical problems.
They impress by:
- Asking good questions
- Spotting risks nobody saw
- Proposing trade-offs clearly
- Making decisions that stick

## Real Talk

Nobody's coming to promote you.

You promote yourself by being someone the team can't function without.

Not because you're the only one who knows the code. But because you make everyone around you better.
