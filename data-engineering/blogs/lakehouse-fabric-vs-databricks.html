Lakehouse Wars: Azure Fabric vs Databricks

A practical comparison for teams choosing their data platform

March 2024 • Architecture, Azure, Databricks

What Even Is a Lakehouse?

A lakehouse combines the low cost & flexibility of data lakes with the reliability & performance of data warehouses. Both Azure Fabric and Databricks are lakehouse platforms, but they take different roads.

Azure Fabric: The Integrated Approach

Microsoft's vision: one unified platform for all data needs (ingestion, transformation, BI, AI). Everything runs on Delta Lake, on top of OneLake (managed storage).

Strengths

  • Tightly integrated with Power BI — semantic models, live connections, instant refresh
  • One bill, one vendor — no shopping between Azure + Databricks + Snowflake
  • Notebook-to-BI workflow is seamless — write transformations, query from Power BI, done
  • OneLake is cheap — pay per GB read/written, not per compute (for storage)
  • Capacity-based pricing — predictable costs. No cluster surprises

Weaknesses

  • Ecosystem is young — fewer third-party integrations than Databricks
  • Compute is limited — Spark SQL works, but Scala/PySpark are less mature
  • Delta Share not production-ready yet — sharing external data is hard
  • Requires Azure stack — if you're multi-cloud, this is a problem

Databricks: The Apache Spark Powerhouse

Databricks is Spark native. You get full control, full language support, and access to the bleeding edge of open source.

Strengths

  • Mature, battle-tested ecosystem — runs Fortune 500 companies' data platforms
  • Multi-cloud — AWS, Azure, GCP. Switch anytime
  • Full Spark power — Scala, Python, SQL, streaming, all first-class
  • dbt integration is seamless — Git-based CI/CD for data transformations
  • AI/ML features — MLflow, Mosaic AI for model serving
  • Delta Sharing works — share live data with partners without copying

Weaknesses

  • Separate BI tool needed — no native BI. You still use Power BI or Looker
  • Cost can surprise you — cluster sprawl, unused instances = runaway bills
  • Requires DBU (Databricks Unit) budgeting — different pricing model per cloud
  • Another vendor to manage — adds operational complexity

Head-to-Head Comparison

Feature Azure Fabric Databricks
Ingestion Data Factory (via Fabric pipelines) Fivetran, Airbyte, custom connectors
Transformation Notebooks, Data Factory pipelines Notebooks, Spark Jobs, dbt
BI Integration Native Power BI (seamless) Via BI Layer (external tool)
Pricing Model Capacity-based (F64, F128, etc.) DBU-based (compute + storage)
Cost Predictability High (fixed capacity) Medium (depends on job behavior)
Multi-Cloud Azure only AWS, Azure, GCP
Maturity 1-2 years old (improving fast) 7+ years, production-hardened
Python/R Support Good Excellent

Decision Framework

Choose Azure Fabric if:

  • You're heavily invested in Microsoft (Teams, Office 365, Azure)
  • You want BI & analytics in one platform
  • You prioritize cost predictability
  • Your team is SQL-focused (less PySpark expertise)
  • You're building first data platform (simpler = faster to value)

Choose Databricks if:

  • You need maximum flexibility and scale
  • Your data scientists need full Python/ML ecosystems
  • You're multi-cloud (AWS + Azure, for example)
  • You need mature streaming (Kafka + Spark Structured Streaming)
  • You want to use modern data tools (dbt, Fivetran, Airflow)

Real Case: When I Chose Each

Project 1: Financial Services (Chose Databricks)

High-volume streaming (100k events/sec), complex ML features, multi-cloud strategy (AWS + Azure). Databricks' streaming engine and MLflow won the day. Cost: justified by handling 50% more data volume efficiently.

Project 2: Retail Analytics (Chose Azure Fabric)

Simple ETL (Data Factory pipelines) + Power BI dashboards for stores. Team was all Azure-trained. Fabric reduced operational overhead (no separate clusters, one vendor). Fast time-to-value.

The Hybrid Future

Most large enterprises don't choose one. They use Fabric for straightforward ELT + BI, Databricks for advanced analytics + ML. The two platforms are converging (Fabric is adding Spark features, Databricks is improving BI).

Key Takeaways

  • Both are legitimate lakehouse platforms. It's not a best-vs-worst question
  • Fabric wins on simplicity & integration. Databricks wins on power & flexibility
  • Cost depends on your data volume, not the platform (well, depends on your discount rate 😄)
  • Lock-in risk is real. Design for portability early (use Delta Lake, dbt, open standards)

Neither platform is wrong. The right choice depends on your team's skills, your company's vendor relationships, and your data complexity.
