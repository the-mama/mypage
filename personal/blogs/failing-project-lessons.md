# What I Learned From Failing a Project

## The Setting

We had 3 months to build a real-time analytics dashboard. Ambitious timeline. Talented team. I was leading it.

We failed. Shipped 6 months late. Users hated it. Needed a complete rewrite.

## The Challenge

I took it hard. I thought I'd failed as a leader. Everyone was disappointed. I considered quitting.

But then I realized something: this failure taught me more than any success.

## The Insight

**Failures are feedback, not judgment.**

Here's what I got wrong:
- I didn't ask enough questions upfront (I assumed)
- I didn't push back on unrealistic timelines
- I didn't involve users until the end
- I prioritized shipping over understanding
- I was afraid to say "this won't work"

## What Changed After

I rebuilt the team's approach:
1. **Talk to users first** — not last
2. **Time estimate + 50%** — timelines are always wrong
3. **Ship small, learn fast** — not big bang releases
4. **Kill ideas early** — before you've invested too much
5. **Celebrate learning over shipping**

The second version shipped in 2 months. Users loved it. Same team, better process.

## How It Shapes Me Today

I'm not afraid of failure anymore. I'm afraid of not learning from it.

Every project that goes wrong is a data point. If I ignore it, I'm doomed to repeat it.

If I learn from it, I level up.
