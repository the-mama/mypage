# Books I Have Read

This is my personal reading journal: a single place where I record the books I finish and the ideas that remain with me. It is not intended to be a detailed review or summary of every chapter. These are simply my reflections on what each book made me notice, question, or understand differently.

## The Psychology of Money

**Author:** Morgan Housel  
**Theme:** Money, behaviour, patience, and long-term thinking

This book explains that managing money is less about intelligence and more about behaviour. People can understand financial formulas and still make poor decisions because fear, ego, comparison, and impatience influence how they act.

The idea that stayed with me is that financial success is not always about earning the highest return. It is about staying in the game, allowing compounding to work, and maintaining enough room for uncertainty. Wealth is often what we do not see: the money not spent, the unnecessary risk not taken, and the freedom quietly built over time.

It also reminded me that everyone makes financial decisions from a different personal history. What looks irrational from the outside may feel completely reasonable to someone shaped by different experiences.

**What I took from it:** Define what “enough” means, avoid decisions driven by comparison, and value independence more than the appearance of success.

## Autobiography of a Yogi

**Author:** Paramahansa Yogananda  
**Theme:** Spirituality, self-discipline, meditation, and inner awareness

This book is both a personal life story and an introduction to a spiritual way of seeing the world. Yogananda's journey through teachers, seekers, discipline, and meditation presents spirituality as something to be experienced rather than merely discussed.

What interested me most was its emphasis on the inner life. We spend significant energy improving our careers, surroundings, and public identity, but rarely give the same attention to understanding the mind. The book suggests that calmness, concentration, and self-knowledge require regular practice, just like any professional skill.

Some experiences described in the book are extraordinary and invite personal interpretation. Even so, its central message is practical: a restless mind cannot find lasting peace by continuously searching outside itself.

**What I took from it:** Meditation is not an escape from ordinary life. It is a way to approach life with greater clarity, discipline, and awareness.
